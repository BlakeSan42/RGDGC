## CASE SUMMARY FOR THE COURT

### The Central Question

Did the Kingwood Service Association systematically breach its contractual obligation to return budget surpluses to member village associations, enabled by a self-dealing governance structure in which the management company owner sat on the nonprofit's board while managing both the overseer and the overseen?

### The Answer (From Our Evidence)

**Yes.** Fourteen years of IRS Form 990 data prove:

1. **$1,007,694** in cumulative operating surpluses were generated in 10 of 14 years
2. **$0** in documented surplus returns to member associations
3. **$0** in reported officer compensation — while the "Managing Agent" owns the for-profit contractor
4. **$0** in reported rental income — despite 6+ active field leases
5. **55.1%** expense spike in 2024 — unexplained, largest in organizational history
6. **F** rating from the BBB — refused to respond to any complaint
7. **0** responses to contractually-mandated arbitration requests

### What We Seek

1. **$1,415,870** in retained surplus plus prejudgment interest
2. **Independent forensic audit** of KSA finances (2011–2024)
3. **Competitive RFP** for the management contract
4. **Conflict of interest policy** — no vendor on the board
5. **McCormick removed** from the KSA board
6. **Management separation** — no dual village/KSA management
7. **Exemplary damages** for fraud (if proven at trial)
8. **Attorney fees and costs**

### Why This Case Matters

This is not about one nonprofit in Kingwood. This case establishes whether Texas community associations can operate self-dealing governance structures indefinitely, shielded from accountability by:
- Circular control (managing both the overseer and the overseen)
- 501(c)(4) opacity (zero employees, all-outsourced operations)
- Systematic nonresponsiveness (refusing arbitration, complaints, and transparency)

The answer should be **no**.
