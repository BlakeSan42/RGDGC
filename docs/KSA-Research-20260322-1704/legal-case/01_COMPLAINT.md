## V. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. The KSA Structure

12. Kingwood is a master-planned community of approximately 23,354 equivalent housing units organized into approximately 29 village community associations and commercial associations (collectively, "Member Associations").

13. KSA was created in 1976 by Friendswood Development Company (an Exxon subsidiary) to manage common areas, parks, public safety, and community entryways as Kingwood expanded. KSA now owns and operates five parks totaling 356.6 acres: River Grove Park (74 acres), East End Park (158.5 acres), Deer Ridge Park, Northpark Recreation Area, and Creekwood Nature Area.

14. KSA's governing documents include Articles of Incorporation, Bylaws, and individual Service Contracts with each Member Association. Under these Service Contracts, KSA provides parks maintenance, entryway maintenance, public safety coordination, and related community services in exchange for quarterly pro-rata assessments from Member Associations based on their share of "equivalent units."

15. The KSA Board of Directors consists of representatives appointed by each Member Association. The Board meets quarterly (third Thursday of January, April, July, and October). The annual budget is proposed in September and voted upon at the October board meeting.

### B. The Management Contract — Structural Self-Dealing

16. For a period spanning decades, KSA has retained Defendant KAM as its management company. KAM's owner, Defendant McCormick, simultaneously holds the following positions:

   a. **Owner and principal of KAM** — the for-profit company receiving the management contract;

   b. **"Managing Agent" on the KSA Board of Directors** — the nonprofit board that awards the management contract;

   c. **Registered Agent of KSA** — the nonprofit's legal representative with the Texas Secretary of State;

   d. **Manager of multiple Member Association village HOAs** — the entities that appoint representatives to the KSA Board.

17. McCormick's simultaneous occupancy of all four positions creates a **circular control structure** in which:

   a. McCormick (through KAM) manages the village HOAs that appoint KSA Board members;

   b. McCormick sits on the KSA Board that awards the management contract to KAM;

   c. KAM receives the management contract, from which McCormick derives private economic benefit;

   d. No independent party exists within the governance structure to challenge or audit this arrangement.

18. Both KSA (the nonprofit) and KAM (the for-profit management company) operate from the **same physical address**: 1075 Kingwood Drive, Suite 100, Kingwood, Texas 77339.

19. The Better Business Bureau lists "Kingwood Association Management" as an **alternate business name** for Kingwood Service Association, further demonstrating the functional interchangeability of the two entities.

20. KSA reports **zero employees** on its IRS Form 990 for all years on record. All operational functions — financial management, vendor coordination, park maintenance oversight, K-sticker administration, event management, and correspondence — are performed by KAM employees under the management contract.

21. Despite functioning as the de facto executive of KSA, McCormick reports **$0 in compensation** from KSA on Form 990 Part VII for all years on record. McCormick's compensation flows exclusively through the KAM management contract, the amount of which is not disclosed on the publicly available portions of KSA's Form 990 filings.

22. On information and belief, the KAM management contract was awarded without competitive bidding, without a public Request for Proposals, and without evaluation of alternative management companies.

### C. Article VIII — Retained Budget Excesses

23. Article VIII of the Service Contract between KSA and each Member Association, titled "Budget Excesses," provides that **remaining unspent assessment amounts at fiscal year end shall be returned to the Member Associations** on a pro-rata basis.

24. Despite this contractual obligation, KSA has systematically retained surplus funds at fiscal year end rather than returning them to the Member Associations.

25. IRS Form 990 filings establish the following surplus years (revenue minus expenses):

26. The cumulative retained surplus of approximately **$1,007,694** (from surplus years alone) constitutes funds belonging to the Member Associations under Article VIII. KSA's retention of these funds is a breach of contract.

27. KSA's net assets grew from $2,913,747 (2011) to $3,637,007 (2023) — an increase of **$723,260** — while the governing documents required surplus return. This net asset growth is direct evidence of systematic retention.

### D. The 2024 Expense Anomaly

28. In fiscal year 2024, KSA's total expenses were **$1,565,650** — a 55.1% increase over the prior year ($1,009,164) and the highest single-year expense in the organization's recorded history.

29. The 14-year median expense level is approximately $845,000. The 2024 expense of $1,565,650 exceeds the median by approximately **85%**.

30. This expense spike caused a net loss of **$516,729**, the largest deficit in KSA's history, reducing net assets from $3,637,007 to $3,120,278 and liquid reserves from $2,594,104 to $2,092,894 — a cash drawdown of **$501,210** in a single year.

31. On information and belief, the specific causes of this 55% expense increase have not been disclosed to Member Associations or the public.

32. The timing of this expense spike — occurring in the same period as the Mills Branch Village lawsuit alleging financial fraud — raises the question of whether the increased expenditures include legal fees, settlement reserves, or other costs related to defending against allegations of misconduct.

### E. The McCormick Departure and Return

33. At a point during the management contract, McCormick and/or KAM departed from managing certain Kingwood communities. **FirstService Residential**, a publicly-traded, nationally-recognized professional community association management company, assumed management.

34. McCormick subsequently **"swiftly returned to reclaim control"** of the communities, displacing FirstService Residential.

35. On information and belief, this transition back to KAM was not preceded by a competitive bidding process, a public RFP, or a transparent evaluation of management alternatives.

36. On information and belief, the village HOA boards that voted to return to KAM were themselves managed by KAM at the time or prior to the transition — further evidencing the circular control structure.

### F. BBB F Rating and Pattern of Nonresponse

37. The Better Business Bureau has assigned KSA a rating of **"F"** — the lowest possible rating — based on KSA's failure to respond to any of the consumer complaints filed against it.

38. This F rating, combined with KSA's refusal to respond to Mills Branch Village's arbitration requests, demonstrates a **systematic pattern of refusing to engage with accountability mechanisms**.

## VI. CAUSES OF ACTION

### COUNT I: BREACH OF CONTRACT (Against KSA)

39. Plaintiffs incorporate all preceding paragraphs by reference.

40. The Service Contract between KSA and each Plaintiff Member Association constitutes a valid and enforceable contract under Texas law.

41. Article VIII of the Service Contract requires KSA to return remaining unspent assessment amounts to Member Associations at the end of each fiscal year.

42. KSA breached Article VIII by systematically retaining surplus funds rather than returning them to Member Associations.

43. Article XI of the Service Contract requires disputes to be resolved by arbitration. KSA breached Article XI by willfully and knowingly refusing to respond to Plaintiffs' multiple arbitration requests.

44. As a direct and proximate result of these breaches, Plaintiffs have suffered damages in an amount to be proven at trial, but believed to exceed **$1,000,000** in cumulative retained surplus funds.

### COUNT II: BREACH OF FIDUCIARY DUTY (Against KSA Board Defendants)

45. Plaintiffs incorporate all preceding paragraphs by reference.

46. As directors and officers of a Texas nonprofit corporation, the Board Defendants owe fiduciary duties to KSA and its Member Associations, including:

   a. **Duty of Loyalty** — to act in good faith and in a manner the director reasonably believes to be in the best interests of the corporation, and not to allow personal interests to prevail over the interests of the corporation. Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code §22.221.

   b. **Duty of Care** — to act with the care that an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances. Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code §22.221.

   c. **Duty of Obedience** — to follow the corporation's governing documents and applicable law.

47. The Board Defendants breached their fiduciary duties by:

   a. Allowing McCormick — the owner of the management contractor — to serve on the KSA Board without adopting a conflict of interest policy, without requiring disclosure, and without requiring recusal from votes related to the management contract;

   b. Awarding the management contract to KAM without competitive bidding;

   c. Failing to ensure compliance with Article VIII's surplus return requirements;

   d. Approving the anomalous 2024 expense increase without adequate disclosure to Member Associations;

   e. Failing to respond to BBB complaints and arbitration requests;

   f. Permitting the functional interchangeability of KSA (the nonprofit) and KAM (the for-profit) as evidenced by the shared address, shared managing agent, and BBB alternate name listing.

### COUNT III: SELF-DEALING / INTERESTED TRANSACTION (Against McCormick and KAM)

48. Plaintiffs incorporate all preceding paragraphs by reference.

49. McCormick, as a director/officer of KSA with a direct financial interest in KAM, engaged in **interested transactions** by:

   a. Participating in KSA Board deliberations and/or votes concerning the management contract from which McCormick derives personal economic benefit through KAM;

   b. Using her position on the KSA Board to ensure the continued award of the KAM management contract without competitive bidding;

   c. Simultaneously managing the village HOAs that appoint KSA Board members, creating a circular governance structure that insulates the KAM contract from challenge.

50. Under Texas law, self-dealing transactions are voidable when they are not fully disclosed and approved by disinterested directors. Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code §22.230.

51. On information and belief, the KAM management contract was not the subject of full disclosure of McCormick's personal financial interest, was not approved solely by disinterested directors, and was not the product of a fair process.

### COUNT IV: FRAUD (Against KSA, McCormick, and KAM)

52. Plaintiffs incorporate all preceding paragraphs by reference.

53. KSA, through McCormick and KAM, made material misrepresentations and/or material omissions to Member Associations by:

   a. Representing through the annual budget process that assessments would be managed in accordance with the Service Contract, including Article VIII's surplus return requirement, when in fact surpluses were systematically retained;

   b. Filing IRS Form 990 reporting $0 in officer compensation for McCormick while McCormick received undisclosed compensation through the KAM management contract, which constitutes a material omission on a public disclosure document;

   c. Reporting $0 in rental income on IRS Form 990 while maintaining at least six active field lease agreements with sports organizations, which constitutes either misclassification or material omission;

   d. Characterizing retained surplus funds as "still needed" when no reserve study, capital improvement plan, or other justification was provided.

54. These misrepresentations and omissions were material, were known to be false or made recklessly without regard for truth, were made with the intent that Plaintiffs rely on them (by continuing to pay assessments without demanding surplus return), and Plaintiffs did in fact rely on them to their detriment.

### COUNT V: UNJUST ENRICHMENT (Against KAM and McCormick)

55. Plaintiffs incorporate all preceding paragraphs by reference.

56. KAM and McCormick have been unjustly enriched by:

   a. Receiving management fees from KSA funded by assessments collected from Member Associations under a contract that was not competitively bid and was tainted by McCormick's self-dealing position;

   b. Receiving management fees from individual village HOAs while simultaneously managing the master association that oversees those same villages, thereby collecting fees from both sides of the oversight relationship;

   c. Benefiting from the retention of surplus funds in KSA's accounts (which increased the budget base and potentially the management fee) when those funds should have been returned to Member Associations under Article VIII.

57. It would be unjust and inequitable for KAM and McCormick to retain these benefits.

### COUNT VI: DECLARATORY JUDGMENT

58. Plaintiffs incorporate all preceding paragraphs by reference.

59. An actual controversy exists between Plaintiffs and Defendants regarding:

   a. Whether KSA has complied with Article VIII's surplus return requirements;

   b. Whether McCormick's simultaneous service as KSA Managing Agent and KAM owner constitutes a prohibited self-dealing arrangement;

   c. Whether the KAM management contract is valid given the circumstances of its award;

   d. Whether KSA waived its right to compel arbitration by refusing to participate in arbitration when requested by Plaintiffs.

60. Plaintiffs seek a declaratory judgment pursuant to Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code, Chapter 37.
