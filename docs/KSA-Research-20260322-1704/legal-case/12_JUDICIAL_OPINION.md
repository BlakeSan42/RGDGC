## I. PRELIMINARY ASSESSMENT — STRENGTHS AND WEAKNESSES OF THE CASE

Before addressing the merits, this Court observes that the quality of the case depends heavily on what discovery reveals. The publicly available evidence establishes a strong prima facie case on breach of contract and self-dealing, but certain elements require the production of internal records to move from "suspicious" to "proven."

### What Makes This Case Strong

**1. Article VIII is unambiguous.** The contractual language — "remaining unspent assessment amounts at fiscal year end shall be returned" — is a mandatory obligation, not discretionary. KSA's "still needed" defense requires the Court to read an exception into the contract that does not exist.

**2. The 990 data is the plaintiff's best friend.** Fourteen years of IRS filings establish the surplus amounts with the reliability of sworn tax documents. KSA cannot credibly dispute its own 990 data. The cumulative surplus of $1,007,694 from positive years is calculable to the dollar.

**3. The structural self-dealing is visible without discovery.** McCormick owns KAM, sits on the KSA board, and serves as registered agent — all confirmed on the 990 and Secretary of State records. No discovery is needed to establish the conflict.

**4. KSA's own conduct undermines its credibility.** Refusing arbitration when contractually required, refusing BBB complaints, and earning an F rating create a pattern of evasion that juries find deeply unpersuasive.

### What Makes This Case Weaker

**1. No direct evidence of intentional fraud (yet).** The complaint alleges fraud, which requires proof of knowing misrepresentation. Structural red flags and circumstantial evidence are strong, but without internal emails or testimony showing intent, the fraud count depends on the discovery phase.

**2. The "prudent reserves" argument has surface appeal.** A juror might find it reasonable for an organization managing 356 acres and $3M in assets to maintain reserves, even if the contract says otherwise. Plaintiffs must hammer the contract language.

**3. The 2024 expense spike is unexplained in both directions.** It could be legitimate capital expenditure just as easily as it could be suspicious. Without the underlying invoices, both sides are speculating.

**4. Damages from excess management fees are speculative without the contract.** Until we know what KAM actually charges, the "excess fee" calculation is an estimate, not a fact.

## II. FINDINGS OF FACT

### The KSA Structure

1. Kingwood Service Association is a Texas nonprofit corporation organized under 26 U.S.C. §501(c)(4), EIN 74-1891991, chartered September 13, 1976.

2. KSA owns and operates five parks totaling 356.6 acres in Kingwood, Texas. Revenue is derived primarily from quarterly pro-rata assessments billed to 29 Member Associations.

3. KSA has zero employees. All operational functions are performed by Kingwood Association Management, Inc. ("KAM"), a for-profit corporation owned by Ethel McAnulty McCormick.

4. McCormick simultaneously serves as (a) owner of KAM; (b) "Managing Agent" on the KSA Board of Directors; (c) KSA's registered agent; and (d) manager of multiple KSA Member Association village HOAs.

5. KSA and KAM share the same physical address: 1075 Kingwood Drive, Suite 100, Kingwood, Texas 77339.

### Article VIII and Surplus Retention

6. Article VIII of the Service Contract between KSA and each Member Association requires KSA to return remaining unspent assessment amounts to Member Associations at the end of each fiscal year.

7. IRS Form 990 filings establish that KSA generated operating surpluses in 10 of the 14 documented fiscal years (2011–2024), totaling $1,007,694.

8. Net assets grew from $2,913,747 (2011) to a peak of $3,637,007 (2023), an increase of $723,260. This growth is consistent with, and probative of, systematic surplus retention.

9. No credible evidence has been presented that any surplus was returned to any Member Association in any year.

10. When Mills Branch Village demanded return of surplus funds in December 2022, KSA refused, characterizing the funds as "still needed."

### Self-Dealing

11. The Court finds that McCormick occupies multiple conflicting roles creating a **structural conflict of interest**. She derives private economic benefit from the management contract her board position helps award.

12. No evidence has been presented that the KAM management contract was the result of competitive bidding at any time.

13. No evidence has been presented that KSA has a written conflict of interest policy.

14. No evidence has been presented that McCormick has recused herself from any Board deliberation or vote concerning the management contract.

15. KAM manages both KSA and multiple KSA Member Association village HOAs. The village HOAs appoint representatives to the KSA Board. This creates a **circular governance structure** where the management company influences the appointment of the board members who award its contract.

### Pattern of Nonresponsiveness

16. The Better Business Bureau has assigned KSA a rating of "F" based on KSA's failure to respond to consumer complaints.

17. Mills Branch Village sent multiple written arbitration requests to KSA during 2023, as required by Article XI of the Service Contract. KSA failed to respond to any request.

18. KSA subsequently sought to compel arbitration only after being sued.

### 2024 Expense Anomaly

19. In fiscal year 2024, KSA's total expenses were $1,565,650 — a 55.1% increase over the prior year and the highest on record. The cause of this increase has not been disclosed.

20. This expense spike resulted in a net loss of $516,729, reducing KSA's liquid reserves by approximately $501,210 in a single year.

## III. CONCLUSIONS OF LAW

### Count I: Breach of Contract — JUDGMENT FOR PLAINTIFFS

21. The Service Contract constitutes a valid and enforceable agreement between KSA and each Member Association.

22. Article VIII's language is unambiguous: "remaining unspent assessment amounts at fiscal year end **shall** be returned." The word "shall" is mandatory under Texas law. *See Helena Chem. Co. v. Wilkins*, 47 S.W.3d 486, 493 (Tex. 2001) ("shall" imposes a duty).

23. KSA's defense that funds were "still needed" fails because Article VIII contains no exception for reserves, contingencies, or anticipated future needs. If KSA intended to maintain a reserve fund, it should have sought an amendment to Article VIII — not unilaterally overridden it.

24. KSA breached Article VIII by retaining surplus funds in 10 fiscal years totaling $1,007,694.

25. KSA further breached Article XI by refusing to participate in arbitration when requested by Mills Branch Village. This constitutes both an independent breach and a waiver of KSA's right to compel arbitration. *See Perry Homes v. Cull*, 258 S.W.3d 580 (Tex. 2008).

26. **Damages: $1,007,694 in retained surplus, plus prejudgment interest of $408,176, totaling $1,415,870.**

### Count II: Breach of Fiduciary Duty — JUDGMENT FOR PLAINTIFFS

27. The Board Defendants, as directors of a Texas nonprofit corporation, owe fiduciary duties of loyalty, care, and obedience to KSA and its Member Associations under Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code §22.221.

28. The Board breached the duty of loyalty by permitting McCormick — the owner of the management contractor — to serve on the Board without implementing a conflict of interest policy, requiring disclosure, or mandating recusal.

29. The Board breached the duty of care by failing to conduct competitive bidding for the management contract and by failing to monitor compliance with Article VIII.

30. The Board breached the duty of obedience by failing to comply with Article VIII's mandatory surplus return requirement.

31. These breaches are encompassed within the contract damages awarded under Count I. However, the fiduciary breach supports equitable remedies including court-ordered governance reforms.

### Count III: Self-Dealing — JUDGMENT FOR PLAINTIFFS

32. McCormick's simultaneous role as KAM owner and KSA Board member constitutes an "interested transaction" under Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code §22.230.

33. The transaction was not the product of full disclosure, disinterested approval, or demonstrable fairness to the corporation. The management contract is therefore **voidable**.

34. The Court orders that the KAM management contract be subjected to a **competitive RFP process within 90 days** of this judgment.

35. The Court further orders that McCormick **shall not serve on the KSA Board in any capacity** while KAM or any entity she controls holds the management contract.

### Count IV: Fraud — JUDGMENT RESERVED

36. The fraud count requires proof of knowing misrepresentation. While the circumstantial evidence is substantial — $0 reported compensation while being paid through KAM, $0 rental income with active leases, characterizing retained surplus as "still needed" — the Court finds that on the current record, the evidence is **sufficient to survive summary judgment** but would require full trial to adjudicate.

37. **The Court strongly recommends** that the parties resolve the fraud count through the settlement framework outlined herein, as a full trial on fraud would be expensive and would expose KSA to exemplary damages that could render the organization insolvent.

### Count V: Unjust Enrichment — JUDGMENT FOR PLAINTIFFS (Partial)

38. KAM has been enriched by receiving management fees from KSA under a contract tainted by self-dealing. However, the Court declines to order disgorgement at this time, as the exact amount of excess fees cannot be determined without a competitive market comparison.

39. The competitive RFP ordered under Count III will establish the market rate. If the new management fee is materially lower than KAM's fee, KSA may seek disgorgement of the excess in a subsequent proceeding.

### Count VI: Declaratory Judgment — GRANTED

40. The Court declares that:
   a. Article VIII requires the return of surplus funds and KSA has not complied;
   b. McCormick's simultaneous ownership of KAM and service on the KSA Board constitutes a self-dealing arrangement;
   c. KSA waived its right to compel arbitration;
   d. The KAM management contract is voidable as an interested transaction.

## IV. JUDGMENT AND REMEDIES

### Monetary Judgment

| Category | Amount |
|----------|--------|
| Retained surplus (Article VIII — 10 years) | $1,007,694 |
| Prejudgment interest (5% per annum, calculated from each year of breach) | $408,176 |
| **Total monetary judgment** | **$1,415,870** |

*Note: Attorney fees to be determined by separate motion. Exemplary damages reserved for trial on fraud count if not settled.*

### Equitable Remedies — COURT ORDERS

The Court orders the following governance reforms:

**Order 1: Independent Forensic Audit**
KSA shall engage an independent CPA firm (not selected by KAM or McCormick) to conduct a forensic audit of KSA's finances for fiscal years 2019–2024. The audit shall be completed within 120 days and the results published to all Member Associations.

**Order 2: Competitive Management RFP**
KSA shall issue a public Request for Proposals for the management contract within 90 days. Minimum three qualified bidders. The winning bidder shall be selected by a vote of disinterested Board members. McCormick shall recuse from all deliberations and voting.

**Order 3: Conflict of Interest Policy**
KSA shall adopt a written conflict of interest policy within 60 days, requiring:
- Annual disclosure of financial interests by all Board members
- Recusal from votes involving personal financial interest
- No person with a financial interest in a KSA vendor may serve on the Board

**Order 4: McCormick Board Removal**
McCormick shall immediately vacate her position on the KSA Board. She may not serve on the Board in any capacity while KAM or any entity she controls holds a management contract with KSA.

**Order 5: Article VIII Compliance (Ongoing)**
KSA shall calculate and return any budget surplus to Member Associations within 90 days of each fiscal year end. Annual surplus calculations shall be independently verified by the CPA firm engaged under Order 1.

**Order 6: Annual Independent Audit (Ongoing)**
KSA shall engage an independent CPA firm to conduct an annual audit, with results published to all Member Associations within 120 days of fiscal year end. Cost: approximately $12,000–$15,000/year.

**Order 7: Management Separation**
The KSA management company may not simultaneously provide management services to any KSA Member Association village HOA. This requirement takes effect upon the next management contract award.

## V. GUIDANCE TO THE PARTIES ON SETTLEMENT

The Court strongly urges the parties to settle the remaining claims (fraud count, attorney fees, exemplary damages) based on the following analysis:

### For Defendants

The monetary judgment of $1,415,870 represents approximately 45% of KSA's total assets ($3,123,778) and 68% of liquid reserves ($2,092,894). Payment will severely strain KSA's ability to operate. If the fraud count proceeds to trial and exemplary damages are awarded, KSA faces potential insolvency.

Further, if other Member Associations file similar claims — and this judgment creates strong incentive for them to do so — aggregate exposure could exceed KSA's total assets.

Settlement is in KSA's existential interest.

### For Plaintiffs

A judgment that renders KSA insolvent serves no one. The parks — including River Grove Park — exist for the community's benefit. Plaintiffs should consider a structured settlement that allows KSA to continue operating while implementing reforms:

| Component | Suggested Settlement |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Cash payment (structured over 3 years) | $1,000,000 |
| Governance reforms (Orders 1–7 above) | Non-negotiable |
| Attorney fees | $300,000 |
| McCormick resignation from Board | Immediate |
| Fraud count dismissed | Upon full compliance |

## VI. JUDICIAL COMMENTARY — WHAT THIS CASE REVEALS

This case reveals a governance failure that is, unfortunately, common in community associations across Texas. The arrangement — a management company owner serving on the board of the nonprofit that awards the management contract, while simultaneously managing the entities that oversee that nonprofit — is a textbook conflict of interest.

What makes this case particularly concerning is the **duration** of the arrangement. For over a decade, surplus funds were retained in apparent violation of a clear contractual obligation, while the person best positioned to identify and correct this violation was also the person with the most to gain from its continuation.

The BBB F rating and the refusal to participate in contractually-mandated arbitration are not merely procedural failures — they are evidence of an organization that has become accustomed to operating without accountability.

The Court notes that no individual is accused of stealing money. The parks are maintained. The entryways are landscaped. The public safety coordination continues. But **competent management is not the same as honest management**, and an organization that operates competently while systematically violating its own contractual obligations is no less liable for the breach.

The reforms ordered by this Court are designed not to punish, but to **restore the accountability that the original governance structure was supposed to provide**. Competitive bidding, conflict of interest policies, independent audits, and management separation are not extraordinary measures — they are baseline governance standards that KSA should have adopted decades ago.

## APPENDIX: WHAT WOULD STRENGTHEN PLAINTIFFS' CASE

From the bench, the Court identifies the following areas where Plaintiffs' case could be strengthened before trial on the remaining counts:

### 1. Obtain the KAM Management Contract
**Impact: CRITICAL.** The single most important document. If the fee is above market rate, excess fee damages become quantifiable. If the fee has escalated without competitive review, it corroborates self-dealing. If it contains favorable terms for KAM (termination protection, automatic renewal), it demonstrates the structural problem.

### 2. Depose McCormick on the Departure/Return
**Impact: HIGH.** What happened when FirstService Residential took over? Did FSR identify any issues? Who voted to bring KAM back? Was there a competitive process? If McCormick's return was engineered through her influence on village boards she had previously managed, this is powerful evidence of circular control.

### 3. Obtain Internal Surplus Calculations
**Impact: HIGH.** If KSA internally calculated surpluses each year (as it should have under Article VIII) and then decided not to return them, those internal memos/emails prove knowledge. If KSA never calculated surpluses, that proves willful blindness.

### 4. Bank Records — Follow the Money
**Impact: HIGH.** Trace every payment from KSA accounts. Identify all payees. Cross-reference with McCormick/Board member relationships. If any vendor is related to McCormick, the self-dealing claim expands significantly.

### 5. 990 Schedule L Analysis
**Impact: MEDIUM-HIGH.** If the KAM contract is not disclosed on Schedule L as a related-party transaction, this is a material omission on a federal tax document — significantly strengthening the fraud count and opening IRS enforcement as a separate pressure vector.

### 6. Expert Testimony on Governance Standards
**Impact: MEDIUM.** An HOA governance expert testifying that competitive bidding, conflict of interest policies, and management separation are industry standards — and that KSA's structure fails every standard — provides the framework for the jury to understand why this matters.

### 7. Other Village Testimonials
**Impact: MEDIUM.** Testimony from non-KAM-managed village board members about what they were told (or not told) about Article VIII surpluses would establish that the nondisclosure was systematic.
