## Year-by-Year Timeline

### Pre-KSA: The Creation of Kingwood (1911–1975)

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1911 | Humble Oil Company chartered in Texas |
| 1917 | Reorganized as Humble Oil and Refining Company |
| 1919 | Jersey Standard (later Exxon) acquires 50% of Humble Oil |
| 1962 | Humble Oil + Del E. Webb Corp form **Friendswood Development Company** |
| 1967 | Friendswood Development (Exxon subsidiary) + **King Ranch** purchase ~14,000 acres from Foster Lumber Company (Dec 28) for planned community |
| 1969–70 | Master plan developed; Kingwood officially conceived as "The Livable Forest" |
| 1971 | **First village (Trailwood) opens**; River Grove Park established as community green space |
| 1972–75 | Additional villages developed; trail system begun; community infrastructure built out |

### 1976 — KSA Founded

- **September 13, 1976:** Kingwood Service Association incorporated in Texas
- Created by Friendswood Development Company to manage common areas, parks, and community services as development expanded
- Initial mission: maintain parks, entryways, trails, and coordinate public safety
- Funded by assessments from member village associations

### 1977–1987 — Growth Era

- Kingwood expands rapidly through the oil boom years
- New villages added, each joining KSA as member associations
- KSA takes ownership of parkland donated by Friendswood Development
- Five parks established: River Grove, East End, Northpark, Deer Ridge, Creekwood Nature Area
- Total parkland grows to 356.6 acres
- 75+ miles of paved hike-and-bike trails connecting villages (maintained by separate Trails Associations)

### 1988 — East End Park / EPA Intervention

- EPA issues cease-and-desist on planned subdivision in what is now East End Park
- Environmental concerns about wetlands and floodplain development
- Land preserved as nature area

### 1991 — Tax-Exempt Status

- **October 1991:** IRS grants KSA 501(c)(4) tax-exempt status
- Formalizes KSA's civic league / social welfare mission
- Assessments from member villages become primary revenue source

### 1994 — East End Park Donation

- Friendswood Development Company donates East End Park land (158.5 acres) to KSA
- Largest single park in the KSA system
- Includes wetlands, boardwalks, and nature trails

### 1995 — Exxon Divests Kingwood

- **Exxon sells Friendswood Development Company to Lennar Corporation for $110 million**
- Severs the Exxon/King Ranch connection to Kingwood
- KSA continues independently as resident-funded nonprofit
- Remaining undeveloped land transitions to Lennar for future village construction

### 1996 — Houston Annexation (Defining Crisis)

- **December 31, 1996 (11:59 PM):** City of Houston forcibly annexes Kingwood
- Residents fought a bitter two-year legal and political battle:
  - Federal lawsuit filed (ultimately unsuccessful)
  - Offered to pay Houston to avoid annexation
  - "Free Kingwood" protest signs proliferated across the community
- **Impact on KSA:**
  - 13 MUDs dissolved; Houston takes over water/sewer/drainage
  - Water bills doubled/tripled as MUD subsidies ended
  - Houston PD replaced independent security arrangements
  - Houston FD replaced the volunteer fire department (11 deaths attributed to unfamiliar responders in first years)
  - **KSA survived** — as a private nonprofit, it was not dissolved by annexation
  - KSA retained ownership of all 5 parks and continued operations
  - KSA's role shifted to parks management + public safety liaison with HPD/HFD

### 1997 — Lake Houston TIRZ Established

- **Lake Houston TIRZ No. 42** created to redirect incremental property tax growth for local infrastructure
- Partial compensation for annexation's impact on community services
- Kingwood falls within Houston City Council District E

### 1999 — Annexation Reform

- Failed de-annexation petition by Kingwood residents
- Texas Legislature passes annexation reform:
  - 3-year service plans required before annexation
  - Public comment periods mandated
  - Arbitration remedies for annexed communities
- Kingwood's experience becomes the cautionary tale that changes Texas law

### 2001–2010 — Steady State Operations

- KSA operates on ~$700K–$850K annual budgets
- Assessment fees stable
- Parks maintained; entryways landscaped
- K-Sticker system enforced for park access
- Community demographics shift as Kingwood matures
- **Kingwood Super Neighborhood Council (#43)** serves as advisory body to Houston city government

### 2013 — River Grove Disc Golf Expansion

- River Grove DGC expanded from 9 holes to 18 holes
- Disc golf community grows in Kingwood
- Course gains regional recognition

### 2014–2016 — Pre-Harvey Stability

- Revenue grows modestly toward $900K
- Assets stable around $3M+
- Assessment fees adjusted incrementally
- Parks maintained in good condition
- River Grove Park heavily used for recreation, disc golf, boating

### 2017 — Hurricane Harvey Devastation

- **August 27–29, 2017:** Hurricane Harvey drops unprecedented rainfall on Kingwood
- **River Grove Park covered by 20 feet of water**
- Damage inventory:
  - 5 feet of sand deposited in parking lot
  - 12-foot-high sand wall (quarter-mile long) blocks western drainage ditch
  - Lagoon near boardwalk filled with sand
  - Soccer fields buried under sand/debris
  - Boat ramp rendered unusable
  - Disc golf course severely damaged
- Recovery begins but is slow and expensive
- Army Corps of Engineers opens drainage ditch (250-foot-wide passage)

### 2018 — Harvey Recovery Continues

- Ongoing debris removal and sand clearing
- Insurance claims and FEMA coordination
- Parks partially reopened as areas are cleared
- Community focus on flood mitigation and prevention

### 2019 — Dredging Vote and Recovery Milestone

- **November 2019:** KSA Parks Committee votes to dredge River Grove Park boat ramp and boardwalk areas
- Mechanical dredging method selected
- Work begins January 2020
- Full restoration projected for March 2020
- **2.5+ years** since Harvey to reach this milestone

## 50-Year Summary

KSA has operated continuously for nearly 50 years (1976–2026) through:

1. **The oil boom growth era** (1976–1987) — rapid village expansion
2. **Environmental preservation** (1988–1994) — East End Park saved
3. **Corporate divestiture** (1995) — survived Exxon selling Kingwood
4. **Forced annexation** (1996) — survived Houston absorption
5. **Steady-state operations** (2001–2016) — stable $700K–$1M budgets
6. **Harvey catastrophe** (2017–2020) — survived and rebuilt
7. **Post-COVID growth** (2020–2023) — $1M+ revenue, growing parks
8. **Financial stress** (2024) — first major deficit, pending litigation

The organization has maintained $3M+ in assets throughout, carries virtually no debt, and has never had paid employees — operating entirely through volunteer governance and contracted management.
