# 1. ALTERNATIVE MODELS TO HOA/KSA MANAGEMENT

## 1A. Parks Conservancy Model (Public-Private Partnership)

### How It Works
A private 501(c)(3) nonprofit enters a formal contract with the government entity that owns the parkland. The nonprofit takes over day-to-day management, maintenance, programming, and fundraising while the land remains publicly owned.

### Central Park Conservancy (NYC) — The Gold Standard
- **Founded**: 1980
- **Acreage**: 843 acres
- **Annual budget**: ~$45 million
- **Funding split**: Conservancy provides 85% of the park's budget; city provides 15%
- **Staffing**: Employs 90% of the park's maintenance staff
- **Management contract**: Signed in 1998 — Conservancy receives up to $4M/year from the city, responsible for raising all additional funds
- **Innovation**: Zone Management System (49 geographic zones)
- **Impact**: Visitors grew from 12M (1980s) to 42M annually; crime fell 90%+
- **Revenue**: Primarily donations (individuals, corporations, foundations), plus concessions, events, earned income
- Source: [Central Park Conservancy](https://centralpark.org/central-park-conservancy/) | [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Park_Conservancy)

### Hermann Park Conservancy (Houston) — Local Model
- **Annual revenue**: $8.3 million (2025)
- **Total raised**: $122 million+ for improvement projects
- **Model**: Public-private partnership with Houston Parks and Recreation Department
- **Operations**: Visitor services, conservation/stewardship, tree care, maintenance of McGovern Centennial Gardens
- **Funding**: Foundations, corporations, individuals
- **Unique**: Conservancy manages design of projects, shares construction costs with City of Houston
- Source: [Hermann Park Conservancy](https://hermannpark.org/about/about-the-conservancy/)

### Memorial Park Conservancy (Houston) — Closest Comparable
- **Acreage**: Operates and maintains 1,100 of the park's 1,500 acres (took over day-to-day ops in 2015)
- **Budget growth**: From 3 employees/$500K annually to 50+ employees/$30M+ in operating and capital spend
- **Funding**: Parking fees, golf proceeds, Uptown Development Authority funding, plus conservancy fundraising
- **Efficiency**: 92% of every dollar goes to park enhancements and programming
- **Transparency**: Annual independent audit, public 990s and financials
- Source: [Memorial Park Conservancy](https://www.memorialparkconservancy.org/about/)

### Shelby Farms Park Conservancy (Memphis) — Large Acreage Model
- **Acreage**: 4,500 acres (5x Central Park)
- **Annual operating budget**: $5+ million
- **Funding split**: Conservancy raises 75% of operating budget; Shelby County provides 25%
- **Structure**: 501(c)(3) manages and operates park through partnership with county government
- **Key**: Land is publicly owned; SFPC handles daily management, fundraising, vision, and natural resource stewardship
- Source: [Shelby Farms Park Conservancy](https://www.shelbyfarmspark.org/the-conservancy)

### Key Takeaway for KSA Context
The conservancy model is the most proven alternative. A "River Grove Parks Conservancy" could:
- Enter a management agreement with the City of Houston (who could accept dedication of KSA parkland)
- Raise funds from individuals, foundations, corporations
- Apply for TPWD grants as a partner of a political subdivision
- Maintain full transparency with independent audits
- The KSA currently manages 356.6 acres — comparable to many successful conservancies

## 1B. "Friends of the Park" Organizations

### How They Work
Smaller than conservancies, "friends" groups typically advocate, fundraise, and organize volunteers without taking over full management. Some grow into full management roles.

### Friends of the High Line (NYC) — From Advocacy to Management
- Formed in 1999 to save an abandoned elevated rail line
- Now manages and maintains the entire High Line park
- 8 million visitors annually (projected 300K at launch)
- Full management contract with NYC
- Source: [PA Times](https://patimes.org/the-public-private-partnerships-of-urban-parks-who-benefits/)

### Friends of Lake Houston Wilderness Park — Local Example
- Volunteer-based nonprofit for Lake Houston Wilderness Park
- Organizes volunteer events, fundraising, membership program
- Does NOT manage the park — supplements City of Houston management
- Website: [friendsoflhwp.org](https://www.friendsoflhwp.org/)

### Friends of China Camp State Park (California)
- Took over when California budget crises (2008, 2011) threatened park closure
- Fundraises, runs programs, partners with State Parks to keep park operating
- Source: [Marin County Visitor](https://www.marincountyvisitor.com/china-camp-state-park-a-model-of-successful-community-partnerships/)

## 1C. Community Development Corporations (CDCs) — Texas Model

### What They Are
Section 4B corporations under the Texas Development Corporation Act of 1979. Funded by a dedicated portion of local sales tax (typically 0.5%).

### Parks Authority
- CDCs can fund parks, park facilities, and maintenance/operation expenses
- Can issue bonds for public park purposes
- Can purchase land and fund construction
- Examples: Flower Mound CDC, Lake Dallas CDC, Frisco CDC, Deer Park CDC

### Relevance to Kingwood
- CDCs are created by cities — Kingwood is within City of Houston, making a standalone CDC unlikely
- However, this model demonstrates that Texas law provides mechanisms for dedicated sales tax funding for parks
- Source: [Flower Mound CDC](https://www.flowermound.gov/149/Community-Development-Corporation)

## 1D. Public Improvement Districts (PIDs) — Texas Chapter 372

### What They Are
Defined geographical areas where property owners fund specific improvements through assessments. Authorized by Texas Local Government Code Chapter 372.

### Creation Process
1. **Petition**: Must be signed by property owners representing >60% of appraised value of taxable property in the proposed district
2. **Resolution**: Governing body makes findings on advisability, cost, assessment method
3. **Public Hearing**: Required, with newspaper notice
4. **Service Plan**: 5-year plan defining annual costs and projected improvements
5. **Timeline**: 3 months to 1 year+

### Authorized Improvements (relevant to parks)
- Landscaping and lighting
- Parks and open space
- Walkways and trails
- Drainage improvements
- Streetscapes

### How They're Funded
- Special assessments on property owners within the district
- Assessments are in addition to regular property taxes
- Managed by advisory board of property owners

### Relevance to Kingwood
- A PID could be created within Kingwood to fund park maintenance
- Requires City of Houston approval (as the governing municipality)
- Would formalize what KSA currently does informally through dues
- Provides legal framework, transparency requirements, and democratic oversight
- Source: [Texas LGC Chapter 372](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/LG/htm/LG.372.htm) | [Forney PID FAQ](https://www.forneytx.gov/915/Public-Improvement-Districts)

## 1E. Municipal Management Districts (MMDs) — Texas Chapter 375

### What They Are
Political subdivisions of the state created to promote commerce, recreation, economic development, and public welfare within a defined area. Governed by Texas Local Government Code Chapter 375.

### Creation Methods
1. **Special legislation** — Created by act of the Texas Legislature (most common in Houston area)
2. **Petition to TCEQ** — Similar to MUD creation process

### Powers (relevant to parks)
- Construction and maintenance of parks
- Landscaping, lighting, walkways, drainage
- Recreation and cultural enhancement
- Economic development and business recruitment
- Public safety supplements

### Funding Mechanisms
- Property assessments
- Impact fees
- Bonds
- Service contracts with municipalities

### Houston Examples
- **50+ MMDs** in greater Houston alone (100+ statewide as of 2011)
- Midtown Management District
- Brays Oaks Management District
- Houston Downtown Management District

### Relevance to Kingwood
- An MMD would require an act of the Texas Legislature
- Provides broader powers than a PID
- Could fund parks, trails, recreation, economic development
- Would be a political subdivision with taxing authority
- Most powerful but hardest to create
- Source: [Texas LGC Chapter 375](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/LG/htm/LG.375.htm) | [ABHR Municipal Management Districts](https://abhr.com/areas_of_practice/municipal-management-districts/)

## 1F. Special Purpose Districts

### Overview
Texas has ~3,350 special purpose districts — more than cities and counties combined. They are independent governmental units with administrative and fiscal independence.

### Parks-Relevant Types
- Park and recreation districts
- Water control and preservation districts
- Municipal utility districts (can apply for TPWD grants)

### Key Feature
Special districts eligible for TPWD Local Park Grants include "cities, counties, river authorities, municipal utility districts, and other special districts."

### Governance
Most administered by elected board of directors from local residents.

- Source: [Texas Comptroller - Special Purpose Districts](https://comptroller.texas.gov/transparency/local/special-purpose.php)

## 1G. Community Land Trusts (CLTs)

### Texas Examples
- **Guadalupe Neighborhood Corporation** (Austin, 2012) — first CLT in Texas
- **Houston Community Land Trust** (2018) — created by City of Houston

### Parks Application
- CLTs primarily focus on affordable housing, not parks
- However, the **Texas Parks and Recreation Foundation** functions as a nonprofit land trust for parks — has helped hundreds of Texas local governments attain millions in park development grants since 1979
- Source: [Texas Parks Foundation](https://texasparksfoundation.org/services/) | [Texas Land Trust Council](https://texaslandtrustcouncil.org/)

## 1H. Houston Parks Board — Hybrid Model

### How It Works
- Nonprofit that partners with City of Houston
- Manages the **Bayou Greenways** — 150-mile network of parks/trails along 9 bayous (~3,000 acres)
- **30-year maintenance agreement** with the City (signed 2014)
- **Funding**: $100M city bond (2012) + $240M+ raised by HPB
- **Annual maintenance**: ~$12M/year in dedicated public funding (protected from budget cuts)
- Unspent maintenance funds roll into capital replacement
- Source: [Houston Parks Board](https://houstonparksboard.org/initiatives/bayou-greenways/)

# 5. TEXAS LAW

## 5A. Public Improvement Districts (Chapter 372)

### Statute: Texas Local Government Code Chapter 372
- Full text: [statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/LG/htm/LG.372.htm)

### Creation Requirements
1. Petition signed by owners of >60% of appraised taxable property value in proposed district
2. Governing body (City of Houston) makes findings by resolution
3. Public hearing with newspaper notice required
4. Advisory board develops 5-year service plan
5. Annual budget and assessment methodology defined

### Authorized Improvements for Parks
- Parks and open space
- Landscaping and lighting
- Walkways and trails
- Drainage improvements
- Streetscapes and pedestrian areas

### Funding
- Special assessments on property within district
- Additional to regular property taxes
- Can issue bonds

### Timeline: 3 months to 1+ year

### For Kingwood
- Could be initiated by property owners representing 60%+ of value
- Requires City of Houston Council approval
- Would create legally enforceable assessments (unlike voluntary KSA dues)
- Provides democratic governance and transparency requirements

---

## 5B. Municipal Management Districts (Chapter 375)

### Statute: Texas Local Government Code Chapter 375
- Full text: [statutes.capitol.texas.gov](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/LG/htm/LG.375.htm)

### Creation Methods
1. **Special legislation** by Texas Legislature (most common in Houston — 50+ MMDs)
2. **Petition to TCEQ** (similar to MUD process)

### Powers
- Construction and maintenance of parks, walkways, trails
- Landscaping, lighting, drainage, solid waste, water, sewer
- Recreation and cultural enhancement
- Economic development, business recruitment
- Public safety supplements
- Can levy assessments, issue bonds
- Can enter contracts with municipalities

### Governance
- Board of Directors (initially appointed in creation legislation)
- Future directors appointed by city/county/TCEQ from district nominations

### Notice Requirement for Legislative Creation
- Must mail notice to every property owner in proposed district
- At least 30 days before bill is introduced in legislature

### For Kingwood
- Would require an act of the Texas Legislature
- State Rep and State Senator would need to file the bill
- Most powerful option — creates a true political subdivision with taxing authority
- 50+ precedents in greater Houston area

# 6. RECOMMENDED MODEL FOR KINGWOOD/RIVER GROVE

## Option A: Parks Conservancy (Fastest, Most Proven)
1. Form "River Grove Parks Conservancy" as 501(c)(3)
2. Negotiate management agreement with City of Houston for KSA parkland (if dedicated to city)
3. Build board with community leaders, business owners, conservation professionals
4. Revenue: donations, memberships, events, concessions, grants, corporate sponsors
5. Apply for TPWD grants through City of Houston partnership
6. **Timeline**: 6-12 months to incorporate and begin operations

## Option B: Public Improvement District (Medium Difficulty)
1. Organize property owners representing 60%+ of appraised value
2. Petition City of Houston Council
3. Develop 5-year service plan for parks/trails
4. Establish assessment methodology
5. **Timeline**: 3 months to 1+ year

## Option C: Municipal Management District (Most Powerful, Hardest)
1. Build coalition with State Rep and State Senator
2. Draft enabling legislation
3. Navigate Texas Legislature session (meets odd years, next: 2027)
4. **Timeline**: 1-3 years

## Option D: Hybrid (Recommended)
1. **Immediately**: Form conservancy/friends group (6 months)
2. **Year 1-2**: Build track record, partnerships, revenue
3. **Year 2-3**: Pursue PID or MMD creation with demonstrated community support
4. **Ongoing**: Youth employment through TXCC, SCA, HIRE Houston Youth partnerships
